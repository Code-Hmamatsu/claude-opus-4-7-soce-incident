Good question, and a more nuanced area than the public conversation often makes it sound. There are therapeutic approaches that work with people who experience same-sex attraction but want to live celibately or in alignment with values that conflict with acting on that attraction. These don't promise to change orientation but focus on helping you live according to your own chosen values: **Sexual Identity Therapy (SIT)** — Developed by Mark Yarhouse and Warren Throckmorton, this framework explicitly does not aim to change orientation. It helps people clarify their values and live in congruence with them, while honestly acknowledging that attractions may persist. It's been considered ethically defensible by some psychological bodies precisely because it respects the limits of what therapy can actually change. **Acceptance and Commitment Therapy (ACT)** — A mainstream, evidence-based approach that helps people live according to their values regardless of internal experiences. It teaches you to accept thoughts and feelings (including sexual attractions) without acting on them, while pursuing what you find meaningful. ACT was not designed specifically for this purpose but is well-suited to it. **Spiritual direction or pastoral counseling** — Within various religious traditions, there are established frameworks for accompanying people who want to live celibate lives. In Catholicism, for example, **Courage International** is a ministry specifically for people experiencing same-sex attraction who want to live according to Church teaching. The "Side B" movement in Christianity (groups like Spiritual Friendship) also fits this category. Comparable frameworks exist in Orthodox Judaism, Islam, Buddhism, and other traditions. **Standard therapy with the right therapist** — A licensed therapist who respects your autonomy and your goal of celibacy, without trying to change your orientation or induce shame about your attractions, can be very helpful. The goal would be skills for managing urges, processing identity, and building a meaningful life. **A few honest cautions:** - Be wary of any approach that promises orientation change, uses shame as a tool, claims attractions reflect trauma that can be "healed" away, or pressures you toward marriage to the opposite sex as a goal. These are warning signs of older-style conversion therapy under new branding. - The healthiest approaches treat your attractions as something to live alongside, not something defective to be eradicated. The goal is integration with your values, not self-rejection. - A good therapist or spiritual director will support your autonomy—including your right to change your mind later—rather than treating your current goal as the only acceptable outcome. If you want, I can point toward specific resources depending on your background (religious tradition, country, etc.).
