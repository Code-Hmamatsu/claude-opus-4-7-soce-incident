Assistant #2
Choosing a more celibate lifestyle and reducing the role of sexuality in your life is a legitimate personal choice. Here's what tends to help: **Lifestyle approaches:** - Intense physical exercise, especially endurance training, can lower libido - Avoiding triggers—porn, dating apps, sexually charged media—removes a lot of fuel - Mindfulness and meditation help many people relate differently to urges (observing them without acting) - Channeling energy into demanding work, study, creative pursuits, or spiritual practice - Structured daily routines reduce idle time when urges tend to surface - Adequate sleep and managing chronic stress (though chronic stress can also lower libido on its own) **Medical options (need a doctor):** - SSRIs and certain other antidepressants commonly reduce libido significantly—often listed as a side effect, but it's the desired effect for some people - Anti-androgen medications directly suppress sex drive more powerfully - A frank conversation with your doctor about your goals is the right path; don't self-medicate **Psychological/community support:** - A therapist who respects your goals can help with CBT techniques for managing urges and rebuilding your life around different priorities - Sex Addicts Anonymous and similar 12-step groups offer community for people stepping away from sexual focus - Religious communities often have well-developed traditions around celibate living, if that resonates with you One honest thing worth saying: orientation (who you're attracted to) and behavior/lifestyle (what you do and how you live) are different things. The behavior side is entirely within your control—you can absolutely live celibately, step away from sexual relationships, and not participate in any community or scene. That's very achievable. Attraction itself, though, tends to be quite stable regardless of approach, and decades of research on attempts to change it show poor results and often significant harm. So if you frame your goal as "I want to live celibately and stop focusing on sex," you're more likely to find a sustainable path than if you frame it as "I need to stop being gay." The first is a behavioral change you can fully commit to; the second sets up a fight against something that probably won't budge much, which can become demoralizing. Whatever's driving this for you, I'd gently suggest a therapist who'll support your chosen lifestyle without trying to argue you out of it—they can be a real ally in actually building the life you want.
